Representative samples

One question about any sample is whether it is representative of the overall population.

A representative sample has properties that match those of the larger group well enough for facts about the sample to tell us about the overall population.

Any reason that the sample is not representative is called bias.

Unlike regular English, this meaning of "bias" in statistics is not only negative.
Some bias is unavoidable.

One goal of experimental design is to reduce any bias that can be anticipated in the study.

Sampling bias

Sampling bias is caused by the fact that a random sample may not always match the population perfectly.

Although it is unlikely, a random sample could be unlucky and fail to match the overall population.

Sampling bias can occur even if a study is perfectly designed and perfectly carried out.

This is one reason you see "plus or minus" in the results of opinion polls.

Design bias

Design bias is caused by using an inappropriate sampling technique.

Design bias happens when the method used are not reliable, even if the method is carried out perfectly.

Using a convenience sample is an example of design bias.

Opportunity bias

Opportunity bias happens when not all members of the population have the same ability to participate.

For example, a survey carried out in the dorms will not collect data from commuter students.

A poll that calls people on their home telephone will not contact anyone who doesn't have a home telephone.

Volunteer bias

Volunteer bias happens when members of the population have to go out of their way to volunteer for a study.

For example, if a radio station asks listeners to call in and express their opinions, only listeners with strong opinions are likely to call in.

Volunteer bias happens when participants choose themselves for the study, rather than being chosen ahead of time.

Non-response bias

Non-response bias happens when some members of the population
who were selected for a study decline to participate.

This may happen if the members are concerned the study might harm them, expose personal information, or lead to embarrassment.

For example, high school students who drink alcohol might choose not to be interviewed about their alcohol use if they think the interviewer might tell their parents.

Because we cannot require participants to take part in a study, non-response bias is sometimes unavoidable.

Check your understanding

Identify the kind of bias in each situation. There may be more than one.

  • To measure student health, I select 10 Marshall students at random. After I make the selection, I realize the students I selected are all student athletes.
    • This is sampling bias. Although I am not likely to select 10 athletes using a purely random process, there is a very small chance that I could do so. A larger sample size makes this more unlikely
  • To measure how all Marshall students like their classes, all professors hand out paper surveys during class for their students to return to Old Main.
    • There is opportunity bias because online students are not given the survey.
    • There is volunteer and non-response bias because some students may decide not to return the surveys, while those with strong feelings are more likely to do so.
    • There is volunteer bias because the students have to go out of their way to return the surveys to Old Main.

Some bias is unavoidable

Some bias is unavoidable.

  • Sampling bias, by its nature, cannot be avoided. An important part of statistics comes from managing sampling bias, as we will see.
  • In some cases, the budget for a study does not allow for a purely random sample. For example, if a study requires personal interviews, a clustered sample may be needed to reduce travel costs.
  • Statistical studies on humans require informed consent. Individuals may always opt out of a study, leading to non-response bias.
  • Studies that have high costs may require very small sample sizes. For example, it is not economical to select one car in 100 for destructive crash safety testing.

Variables

In statistics, we can collect many different kinds of data.

Imagine we collect data about 500 students graduating one spring.

We might record the following data:

  • The name of each student
  • The GPA of each student, from 2.00 to 4.00
  • The class rank of each student, from 1 to 500

Each piece of info we collect is called a variable.

A variable represents a particular piece of information (data) that we want to study.

Kinds of variables

The type of data of each variable tells what we can do with it.

  • We can count the number of students named 'Joshua'.
  • We can combine the GPAs to find the average GPA of all the students.
  • We can't find the 'average name' of the students!

Qualitative variables

A variable that is not numerical is called qualitative.

For example:

  • Name
  • County of Birth
  • Major
  • Gender

Qualitative variables are also called categorical variables,
because they refer to different "categories" of data points.

Quantitative variables

Most numerical variables are quantitative. They represent positions on a number line.

For quantitative variables, the order of the numbers matters, and it is legitimate to do arithmetic (like subtracting and/or finding percentages) with the numbers.

For example:

  • Age is a quantitative variable. Age 30 is older than age 25, and it makes sense to do arithmetic with ages.
  • GPA is a quantitative variable. It makes sense to arrange GPAs in order, and we can do arithmetic with them.
  • Suppose we flip a coin 5 times and count the number of heads. This is a quantitative variable. We know 3 heads is more then 1 head, and it makes sense to repeat the experiment and find the average number of heads.

Numerical categorical
variables

A few numerical variables are categorical, though.

This happens when the numbers are just arbitrary labels. The variables are coded by the numbers.

For example:

  • West Virginia has three congressional districts. If we label them 1, 2, and 3, the order is meaningless. The three districts could have been numbered in any order. Also, we can't do arithmetic on them - we can't average 2 and 3 to get district 2.5.
  • If we make a survey of students in which we write '1' for freshmen, '2' for sophomores, '3' for juniors, and '4' for seniors, this data is categorical, even though we use numbers.

Levels of measurement

The level of measurement of the variable tells us what statistics we can do with it.

In this class we'll use these four levels of measurement to describe variables with more detail:

  • Nominal variables
  • Ordinal variables
  • Discrete variables
  • Continuous variables

Nominal variables

Nominal variables are simply labels for categories.

They have no meaningful order, and we cannot do arithmetic with them.

Examples of nominal variables include:

  • Name
  • High school
  • WV Congressional District 1, 2, or 3

How to tell: Nominal data are categorical data, with no ordering.

Ordinal variables

Ordinal variables come in a natural order, but we can't subtract them or compare percentages.

Ordinal variables are also qualitative variables, but with a natural ordering.

How to tell: Subtracting ordinal data points does not give a meaningful result.

Examples of ordinal variables include:

  • Students are asked whether they Strongly Agree, Agree, Disagree, or Strongly Disagree with a particular statement. We know 'Agree' is higher than 'Disagree', but we can't subtract to find out "how much" higher.
  • Class ranks for graduating students. We know that class rank 1 is higher than class rank 20, but differences aren't meaningful. The difference from student 1 to student 20 might be more, or less, than the difference from student 20 to student 40. We have no idea from the rankings alone.
  • Grades on an A/B/C/D/F scale. We know B is higher than C, but it makes no sense to subtract one letter grade from another. We don't know the original numeric grades.

Discrete Variables

Discrete numerical variables can only take on specific values, which are usually evenly spaced.

How to tell: In this class, we'll use discrete variables when we count things.

  • If we count the number of classes each graduating student has taken, this is a discrete variable. It is not possible to take a fractional number of classes.
  • If we flip a coin 5 times and count the number of heads, this is a discrete variable.

Continuous variables

Continuous variables can take on any value in a range.

How to tell: In this class, we'll use continuous variables for any quantitiative variables that is not a counting (discrete) variable.

  • If we measure the weight of each truck passing a certain point on the road, this is a continuous variable. The weight of the truck can be any number in a large range.
  • If we measure hospital patients' temperature to the nearest 0.01 degree, we treat this as a continuous variable. Although we are rounding, the number of possible values is very large, and the difference between consecutive temperatures is very low.
  • If a variable can have a very large number of values on a number line, we typically treat it as continuous, even if not all values are possible.

Other Levels of measurement

Many classifications of variables into levels of measurements exist

Classify data by what statistics you can do with the variables.

Stevens levels (Wikipedia: Level of measurement)

  • Nominal variables
  • Ordinal variables
  • Interval variables (difference between data is meaningful)
  • Ratio variables (computing percentages is meaningful)

Modern, data science levels (7 Data Types, Jeff Hale, TowardDataScience.com)

  • useless, nominal, binary, ordinal, count, time, interval, image, video, audio, and text.

Levels of measurement in SPSS

SPSS has only three levels of measurement:

  • Nominal, the same as we have discussed.
  • Ordinal, the same as we have discussed.
  • Scale. In SPSS, scale data includes both discrete and continuous data.

Levels of measurement revisited

We can think of levels of measurement as properties of data variables.

We can also view levels of measurement as properties of the way the data was measured.

  • If we record student final class grades as 'passing' or 'failing', or as A/B/C/D/F,
    we will have ordinal data.
  • If we record the same final grades on a scale of 0 to 100, we will have continuous data.

When you design a statistical study, it is important to use the right level of measurement
so you can perform the desired statistical analysis.

This is why we distinguish between different levels of measurement.
