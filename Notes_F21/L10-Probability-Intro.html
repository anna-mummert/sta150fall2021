Probability

Probability is a mathematical tool used to study randomness.

Probability is a measure associated with how certain we are of an outcome of a particular experiment (activity, event).

Probabilities give us a systematic approach to "guess" about the outcome of an event in order to make a decision.

Experiment

A random exeriment is a procedure to obtain an outcome from a collection of possible outcomes.

The set of all possible outcomes is the sample space.

  • In some cases you can write out all possible outcomes.
    • Flip two coins - the possible outcomes are (head, head), (head, tail), (tail, head), (tail, tail).
  • In some cases you cannot list all possible outcomes.
    • Similar to sampling -- too many individuals, too expensive

An event is the set of outcomes you are interested in.

  • What is the probability that when I flip two coins, the result is one head and one tail? (head,tail) or (tail,head)

Probability

A probability is a number between 0 and 1 that tells you how often an event will happen if you peform the experiment many times, the long-term relative frequency.

  • Probabiliy 0 means the event never happens
  • Probabilities closer to 0 mean the event happens less often
  • Probabilities closer to 1 mean the event happens more often
  • Probability 1 means the even always happens

If you can write out all possible outcomes, then you can compute the theoretical (exact) probability of an event.

If you cannot list all possible outcomes, you need to compute the long-term relative frequency of the event. Estimate probability of population from sample probability.

OR events

In mathematics, "or" means one, or the other, or both.

An outcome is in the event "A or B" if the outcome is in A, or the outcome is in B, or the outcome is in both A and B.

Example -- A student at Marshall is a first-generation student (A) or a first year student (B).

Caution! Very often in English "or" means "exclusive or" -- one or the other, but not both.

And events

An outcome is in the event "A and B" if the outcome is in both A and B at the same time.

Example -- A student at Marshall is a first-generation student (A) and a first year student (B).

Multiplication Principle

A compound experiment $E$ is a larger experiment consisting of two smaller experiments $E_1$ and $E_2$ that do not affect each other.

The number of outcomes of $E$ is the product of the number of outcomes of $E_1$ and the number of outcomes of $E_2$.

Example -- $E_1$ flip one coin, $E_2$ flip a second coin.

  • The result of either coin has no affect on the other coin.
  • $n_1 = 2$ and $n_2 = 2$, so the compound experiment has $n = 4$ possible outcomes
    • Flip two coins - the possible outcomes are (head, head), (head, tail), (tail, head), (tail, tail).

Complement events

The complement of event A consists of all outcomes that are not in A.

Example -- event A is "roll a 5 or 6" when rolling one six-sided die. The complement is "roll a 1, 2, 3, or 4" (anthing but a 5 or 6).

Example - a Marshall student is a first year student. The complement event is "a student is not a first year student".

General Probability Formula

\[\text{Probability of an event} = \frac{\text{Number of outcomes in the event}}{\text{Total number of possible outcomes}} \]

What is the probability a Marshall student is an undergraduate student?

Experiment -- determine the undergrad / grad status of each student

Outcome of interest -- undergraduate

In Fall 2019, Marshall had a total of 12,862 students (https://www.marshall.edu/quick-facts/, Accessed 2021.07.05) of which 9,422 were undergraduates.

The probability that a randomly selected student at Marshall, in fall 2019, was an undergraduate is \[P = \frac{9,422}{12,862} = 0.733 = 73.3\% \]

Law of Large Numbers

The law of large numbers states that as the number of repetitions of an experiment is increased, the relative frequency obtained in the experiment becomes closer to the theoretical probability.

Example -- Roll one six-sided die.

The theoretical probability of rolling a 1 (or any other number) is 1/6.

If you roll a six-sided die a lot, the relative frequency will be close to 1/6.

Check your understanding

True or False.

  • An event with probability 0 will never happen.
    • True
  • An event with probability larger than 0 is guaranteed to happen (will certainly happen).
    • False
  • An event A that has a larger probability than event B is more likely to happen.
    • True
  • Given a data set, the relative frequency of an event is the probability of the event.
    • True
  • You could find an event that has probability 3.
    • False
