Probability

A probability is a number between 0 and 1 that tells you how often an event will happen if you peform the experiment many times, the long-term relative frequency.

  • Probabiliy 0 means the event never happens
  • Probabilities closer to 0 mean the event happens less often
  • Probabilities closer to 1 mean the event happens more often
  • Probability 1 means the even always happens

If you can write out all possible outcomes, then you can compute the theoretical (exact) probability of an event.

If you cannot list all possible outcomes, you need to compute the long-term relative frequency of the event.

Basic facts

  • The total probability of the entire spample space (all possible outcomes) is 1
  • For any event, $0 \leq P(A) \leq 1$
  • For any event A with probability $P(A)$, the complement of $A$, $A^c$, has probability $1 - P(A)$ \[P(A^c) = 1 - P(A) \]

Independent events

Two events are independent if the probability of each one occurring does not depend on whether the other one ocurred.

Multiplication rule for independent events: \[P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B) \]

Example -- roll a six-sided die and flip a fair coin. What is the probability that we flip heads and roll a 4?

  • Flipping a coin and rolling a die are independent -- the probability of one does not depend on whether the other occurred.
  • P(flip heads) = 1/2 and P(roll 4) = 1/6
  • P(flip heads and roll 4) = (1/2) * (1/6) = 1/12

Mutually exclusive events

Two events are mutually exclusive if they cannot both occur at the same time.

Two events are mutually exclusive if the probability they both occur at the same time (A and B) is 0 \[P(A \text{ and } B) = 0 \]

Addition rule for mutually exclusive events: \[P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) \]


Example -- Roll a six-sided die. What is the probability that you roll a 2 or an odd number?

  • Rolling a 2 and rolliing an odd number are mutually exclusive -- they cannot occur at the same time.
  • P(roll 2) = 1/6 and P(roll odd) = 1/2
  • P(roll 2 or roll odd) = 1/6 + 1/2 = 4/6 = 2/3

General Addition Rule

For any events $A$ and $B$ \[P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \text{ or } B) \]

Idea: "or" counts events in $A$, in $B$, or in both.

If an event is in both, then it is "double counted" once in $P(A)$ and again in $P(B)$.

Subtract off the overlap ($A$ and $B$) once to remove the double counting.


Example -- Roll a six-sided die. What is the probability that you roll a 2 or an even number?

  • Rolling a 2 and rolling an even number are not mutually exclusive -- they can occur at the same time.
  • P(roll 2) = 1/6 and P(roll even) = 1/2 and P(roll 2 and roll even) = 1/6
  • P(roll 2 or roll even) = 1/6 + 1/2 - 1/6 = 1/2

Complement events

  • Every event is in $A$ or $A^c$. Together they are the entire sample space.
    • $P(A \text{ or } A^c) = 1$
  • $A$ and $A^c$ are mutually exclusive events -- they cannot occur at the same time
    • $P(A \text{ and } A^c) = 0$
    • $P(A \text{ or } A^c) = P(A) + P(A^c) = 1$
  • $A$ and $A^c$ are not independent events -- the probability of one does depend on whether the other occurred
    • $P(A \text{ and } A^c) \neq P(A) \times P(A^c)$

Check your understanding

$A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive events with $P(A) = 0.2$ and $P(B) = 0.3$.

  • Find $P(A \text{ and } B)$.
    • $P(A \text{ and } B) = 0$
  • Find $P(A | B)$.
    • $P(A | B) = \dfrac{P(A \text{ and } B)}{P(B)} = 0$
  • Find $P(A \text{ or } B) $.
    • $P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) = 0.5$
  • Is it possible to have two mutually exclusive events with $P(A) = 0.7$ and $P(B) = 0.4$
    • No. Two mutually exclusive events with these probabilities would have $P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) = 1.1$. No probability can be more than $1$.
