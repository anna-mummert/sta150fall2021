Conditional Probability

A conditional probability is the probability that one event will happen given that we know another event happened.


Example

Roll a six-sided die. If you know you rolled an even number, the probability that we roll a 2 is 1/3.

Without the extra knowledge, the probability that we roll a 2 is 1/6.

Notation

We write P(A|B) for the conditional probability of event A gvien that event B happened. \[P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \text{ and } B)}{P(B)} \]

Example

Roll a six-sided die. If you know you rolled an even number, the probability that we roll a 2 is 1/3. \[P(\text{ roll 2 } | \text{ roll even }) = \frac{P(\text{ roll 2 and roll even })}{P(\text{ roll even })} = \frac{1/6}{1/2} = 1/3\]

Independent Events

If A and B are independent (probability of one does not depend on whether the other occurred), then

\[P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \text{ and } B)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(A)\times P(B)}{P(B)} = P(A)\]

Example -- roll a six-sided die and flip a fair coin. What is the probability that we flip heads given that we rolled a 4?

  • Flipping a coin and rolling a die are independent -- the probability of one does not depend on whether the other occurred.
  • P(flip heads) = 1/2 and P(roll 4) = 1/6
  • P(flip heads and roll 4) = (1/2) * (1/6) = 1/12
  • P(flip heads | roll 4) = (1/2) * (1/6) / 1/6 = 1/2

Mutually exclusive events

If A and B are mutually exclusive (cannot occur at the same time), then

\[P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \text{ and } B)}{P(B)} = \frac{0}{P(B)} = 0\]

Example -- Roll a six-sided die. What is the probability that you roll a 2 given you roll an odd number?

  • Rolling a 2 and rolliing an odd number are mutually exclusive -- they cannot occur at the same time.
  • P(roll 2) = 1/6 and P(roll odd) = 1/2
  • P(roll 2 and roll odd) = 0
  • P(roll 2 | roll odd) = 0 / 1/2 = 0

Contingency Table

A contingency table is a way of displaying data that has been classified with two different categorical variables.

  • One variable corresponds to the rows; one to the columns
  • Cells are "and" probabilities for sample members who are in both categories.
  • Total counts are given for the rows and columns.

Contingency tables are useful when computing condititional probabilities.

Example

The following table shows data about smoking in a population of 500 people.

MenWomenTotal
Smoker 39 37 76
Nonsmoker 191 233 424
Total 230 270 500

The rows correspond to smoking status - smoker or nonsmoker

The columns correspond to gender - men or women

The counts in the boxes are individuals who are in both. 39 individuals from the 500 are both a smoker and a man.

Totals along columns and rows are given

We saw this example in L04 - Percentages and Box Plots. You computed conditional probabilities.

Example continued

  • What percent of the people in the study are men?
    $P(\text{men}) = 230/500 = 0.46 = 46\%$
  • What percent of the people in the study are smokers?
    $P(\text{smoker}) = 76/500 = 0.152 = 15.2\%$
  • What percent of men in the study are smokers?
    $P(\text{smoker } | \text{ men}) = 39/230 = 0.17 = 17\%$
  • What percent of women in the study are smokers?
    $P(\text{smoker } | \text{ women}) = 37/270 = 0.137 = 13.7\%$
  • What percent of smokers in the study are women?
    $P(\text{women } | \text{ smoker}) = 37/76= 0.486 = 48.6\%$
