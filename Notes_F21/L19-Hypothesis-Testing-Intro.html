Hypothesis Testing

We compare a (sample) statistic to a pedetermined value. We know the values are not the same. We want to know if the difference is signficant.


The null hypothesis is that the difference in the numbers is due to chance. Notation: $H_0$.

  • The numbers are not really different. The difference between the two numbers is 0.

The alternative hypothesis (or research hypothesis) is that the difference is not due to chance. The difference is significant. Notation: $H_1$ (or $H_a$).

  • The numbers are really different. The difference between the two numbers is not 0.


Use the sample data as evidence to support one of the two hypotheses.

Null and Alternative Hypotheses

Write the null hypothesis as "the mean equals [value]"

The alternative hypothesis could be

  • "the mean is not equal to [value]"
  • "the mean is greater than [value]"
  • "the mean is less than [value]"


Example: A medical trial is conducted to test wheter or not a new medicine reduces cholesterol by $25\%$.

Null hypothesis: the new medicine reduces cholsterol by $25\%$ \[H_0: \mu = 25\%\]

Alternative hypothesis: the new medicine does not reduces cholsterol by $25\%$ \[H_1: \mu \neq 25\%\]

Outcomes

Use the sample data as evidence to support one of the two hypotheses.

Sample data supports the null hypothesis, we say

  • Do not reject the null hypothesis. Data does not suppport the claim of the alternative hypothesis.

Sample data supports the alternative hypothesis, we say

  • Reject the null hypothesis. Data does suppport the claim of the alternative hypothesis.

Evidence

Use the sample data as evidence to support one of the two hypotheses.

Compute the $z$ value for the sample mean $\overline{x}$ using the

  • hypothesized value as the population mean $\mu$
  • the standard deviation of the sampling mean $\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$
\[z = (\overline{x} - \mu) / \left(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \]

Compute the probability for the $z$-value

  • probability large, then data supports null hypothesis ($\overline{x}$ and $\mu$ are close; hypothesized value $\mu$ is likely)
  • probability small, then data supports alternative hypothesis ($\overline{x}$ and $\mu$ are very different; hypothesized value is unlikely)

Example

The mean work week for engineers in a company is believed to be about 60 hours. A newly hired engineer hopes that it's shorter. The work week of 10 engineers from the company are \[45 \quad 50 \quad 55 \quad 55 \quad 55 \quad 55 \quad 60 \quad 60 \quad 65\quad 70 \] Based on the data should the new engineer count on the mean work week to be shorter than 60 hours?

The length of engineer work weeks at this company is known to be normally distributed with a standard deviation of 6 hours.

Example

\[45 \quad 50 \quad 55 \quad 55 \quad 55 \quad 55 \quad 60 \quad 60 \quad 65\quad 70 \]

Find the sample average. 57 hours

  • The sample average is less than the hypothesized 60 hours. Is it significantly less?

Null hypothesis: $H_0: \mu = 60$ hours

Alternative hypothesis: $H_1: \mu < 60$ hours

Compute the $z$-value \[z = (57 - 60) / \left(\frac{6}{\sqrt{10}}\right) = -1.58 \]

Compute $P(z < -1.58)$ -- use the same "less than" as in the alternative hypothesis \[ P(z < -1.58) = 0.0571 \]

Example

Null hypothesis: $H_0: \mu = 60$ hours

Alternative hypothesis: $H_1: \mu < 60$ hours

We computed the probability as \[ P(z < -1.58) = 0.0571 \]

Does the evidence support the null hypothesis (is $p$ large) or the alternative hypothesis (is $p$ small)?

Typically, you have in mind a preset "level of significance" $\alpha$ that you compare your $p$-value against.

  • If $p > \alpha$ then data supports null hypothesis (do not reject the null hypothesis).
  • If $p < \alpha$ then data supports alternative hypothesis (reject the null hypothesis).

One-sided and Two-sided Tests

One-sided test -- the alternative hypothesis is "less than" or "greater than".

  • For "less than", read the $p$-value directly from the $Z$ table
  • For "greater than", find the probability and subtract : $1 - p$.

Two-sided test -- the alternative hypothesis is "not equal to"

  • If $z < 0$, find the probability and multiply by 2 : $2p$.
  • If $z > 0$, find the probability, subtract, and multiply by 2 : $2(1-p)$.


Hypothesis testing SPSS

  • Always uses the Student's t-test (assumes population standard deviation unknown)
  • Always gives two sided $p$-value.
    • Divide the two sided value by 2, to get the one-sided value

Write the null and alternative hypotheses for each scenario.

  • We want to test whether the mean height of eighth graders is 66 inches.
    • Null hypothesis: The mean height of eighth graders is 66 inches. $H_0: \mu = 66$ inches
    • Alternative hypothesis: The mean height of eighth graders is not 66 inches. $H_1: \mu \neq 66$ inches
  • We want to test if college students take less than five years to graduate from college, on the average.
    • Null hypothesis: On average, college students take five years to graduate from college. $H_0: \mu = 5$ years
    • Alternative hypothesis: On average, college students take less than five years to graduate from college. $H_1: \mu < 5$ years
